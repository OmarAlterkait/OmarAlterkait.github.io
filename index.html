About

I work at the intersection of machine learning and neutrino physics. My research centers on sparse 3D inverse problems: reconstructing particle interactions from raw sensor data in detectors like liquid argon time projection chambers and large optical detectors. To do that, I develop GPU-accelerated differentiable simulators in JAX, achieving 100× to 10,000× speedups over traditional Monte Carlo, and self-supervised foundation models trained on datasets of more than 10 million events.

I am a PhD candidate at Tufts University advised by Taritree Wongjirad, an IAIFI Junior Investigator at MIT, and currently a DOE SCGSR Fellow at SLAC working with Kazuhiro Terao. In July 2026 I will join CERN as an SCGSR-IRCA Fellow. Originally from Kuwait, I am an Academic Scholar of Kuwait University, and an Arabic calligrapher when away from the keyboard.

Research

JAXTPC: Multi-modal foundation models for 3D reconstruction in LArTPCs

with Kazuhiro Terao (SLAC)

The first 10-million-event dataset pairing raw sensor waveforms with 3D ground truth, 30× larger than anything existing. A JAX/GPU rewrite brought simulation from 50s to 0.5s per event, capturing charge and light modalities from the same physics events. On top of it: PTv3/Sonata point cloud transformers and cross-modal masked autoencoding directly on raw data, with public Data Olympics benchmarks to drive collaborative algorithm development.

GitHub

LUCiD: Differentiable ray-tracing for optical detector calibration

with César Jesús-Valls (CERN)

The first ray-tracing framework that is end-to-end differentiable from particle parameters to detector response. Neural photon emission models keep stochastic physics differentiable; JAX JIT compilation and grid-based spatial filtering deliver 1M photons in 30ms, a 10,000× speedup over CPU Monte Carlo. Matches state-of-the-art reconstruction baselines while optimizing directly through parameter landscapes where MCMC fails.

GitHub arXiv:2602.24129

Sparse Euclidean equivariant networks for particle reconstruction

with Tess Smidt (MIT)

Combining sparse convolutions with E(3) equivariant architectures so networks respect rotation, translation, and reflection symmetry by construction: no data augmentation and better sample efficiency through geometric inductive biases, targeted at particle reconstruction in the MicroBooNE detector.
